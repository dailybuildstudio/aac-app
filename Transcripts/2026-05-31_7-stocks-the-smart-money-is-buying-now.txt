300 billion dollars of capital just moved. 10 of the most respected investors on Wall Street filed their first quarter trades on May 15th. Buffett's Berkshire, Burry, Ackman, Bridgewater, Pabrai, Howard Marks, Druckenmiller, Tepper, Chase Coleman, and the ex-OpenAI researcher running a 13 billion-dollar fund. They don't agree on a single thing. Berkshire bought Google. Ackman dumped it. Ackman bought Microsoft. Tepper dumped most of his. Burry bought Mercado Libre. Druckenmiller sold 94% of his. Seven stocks expose where the fight is sharpest right now. One of them is a bet almost no one else on the street is willing to make. That's what we're walking through. Start with the alphabet. Greg Abel ran his first full quarter at Berkshire in quarter one, and he made one move that broke 60 years of Buffett tradition. Abel increased Berkshire's Alphabet position by 224%. That puts Berkshire at almost 58 million shares of Google, about 16 billion dollars, the seventh largest position in the entire portfolio. Buffett spent six decades calling tech outside his circle of competence. Apple was the one exception. Abel just made a second. Abel also exited 16 positions outright. Amazon, Visa, Mastercard, United Health, Domino's, all gone. Berkshire's equity book dropped from 42 positions to 29. He sold 16 names he wasn't sure about, and bet 16 billion dollars on one he was. But others were heading the opposite way. Bill Ackman sold 95% of his Alphabet stake. He used the cash to fund a new Microsoft position worth roughly $2 billion, the fourth largest in his entire 13 position book. Ackman explained it on X. The sale wasn't a bet against Alphabet. He wrote that Pershing Square is still very bullish long-term. He used GOOG as a source of funds for Microsoft citing current valuations and the fund's finite capital base. Stanley Druckenmiller went further. He exited every single one of his 385,000 Alphabet shares. And Chase Coleman's Tiger Global still has Alphabet as its single largest holding, 13.38% of a $23 billion portfolio. Five smart money investors, three opposite views on one stock. And Alphabet is the easy one. Take Amazon. Abel exited Berkshire's entire Amazon position in quarter one. The whole thing gone. Berkshire was the loudest seller in the cohort, but not the only one. David Tepper nearly doubled his Amazon stake. Amazon dethroned Alibaba and became his number one position at Appaloosa after Alibaba had held that spot for three straight quarters. Bill Ackman added another 19% to his Amazon position taking it to roughly $2.4 billion, his second largest holding. Ray Dalio's Bridgewater more than doubled its Amazon position adding $508 million in a single quarter. And Stanley Druckenmiller cut his Amazon shares by 99% but doubled his Amazon call options, a bearish trim with a small leveraged hedge in case he's wrong. For Tepper, Amazon is now 15.16% of his entire portfolio. One stock is taking up almost 1/6 of one of the most active hedge funds in the country. So, one sold, three bought, and one did both. These are some of the most experienced capital allocators alive. Buying and selling the same stock in the same quarter at the same prices. Same balance sheet, same earnings, same analyst coverage, opposite conclusions. What does Tepper see that Abel doesn't? Tepper sees a company still growing into its valuation. AWS leads cloud. Retail margins keep expanding. The advertising business has quietly become one of the biggest growth engines in tech. Abel sees a stock that has run a long way in a portfolio that already had heavy tech exposure through Apple. Both reads make sense. That is exactly why one of them is going to be wrong. The next fight is louder still. Microsoft is the cleanest example of that fight. Bill Ackman opened a brand new Microsoft position in Q1. 5.65 million shares, roughly $2 billion. His fourth largest position out of 13 total. For a fund that holds only 13 names, opening a $2 billion new position is the kind of move you make when you have unusually high conviction. But almost everyone else with a Microsoft position was selling. Chris Hohn's TCI fund cut its Microsoft stake from 10% of the portfolio to 1%. TCI had owned Microsoft almost continuously since the fourth quarter of 2017 and it made nearly $19 billion in profit last year, the largest annual hedge fund profit on record. Hohn explained the move in his Q1 shareholder letter. He said the rapid progress in AI introduces uncertainty over Microsoft's competitive position in the future. His concerns went beyond the office productivity franchise. He said TCI also sees risks in Azure. TCI wasn't alone. David Tepper cut his Microsoft stake by 82%. Stephen Mandel's Lone Pine exited completely. Chase Coleman's Tiger Global reduced by 54%. Coatue trimmed by 52%. So, one billionaire put $2 into Microsoft in Q1 and almost every other major Microsoft holder on Wall Street was running for the exit. That isn't a debate about price. It is a fundamental disagreement on whether Microsoft can hold its place in the AI economy. $3 fights so far, all in tech, all in the same handful of names. But the deepest disagreement on this list isn't about which tech stock to own. It's about whether to own tech at all. Outside the trillion-dollar names, the disagreement gets surgical. Take Mercado Libre. On May 9th, Michael Burry did something he rarely does. He posted on his Substack newsletter that he had just opened a new full position in Mercado Libre. He bought in the 1,600s. The morning after Mercado Libre fell nearly 13% on its Q1 2026 earnings report. Burry doesn't normally his trades. When he does, it means he is openly confident. His logic was specific. He cited expectations for roughly $40 billion in Mercado Libre revenue in 2026, up about 30% from 2025. He said the stock had fallen well below his 15-year intrinsic value framework. Chase Coleman's Tiger Global initiated a brand-new Mercado Libre position, too. $233 million, the largest of Coleman's four new positions in quarter one. Two of the sharpest investors on Wall Street with completely different styles opened the same new position at the same time. But the third move on Mercado Libre cuts the other way. Stanley Druckenmiller sold 94% of his Mercado Libre stake in the same quarter. Druckenmiller had owned Mercado Libre for over a year. He took his profits and rotated into something else. Burry calls the stock undervalued. Coleman puts $200 million on it. Druckenmiller unloads 94% of his. Three of the most respected investors alive looking at the same balance sheet reaching three opposite conclusions. This kind of three-way split on a single stock rarely happens at this caliber of investor. When it does, it usually means the market hasn't yet decided what the company is worth. What does Druckenmiller see that Burry doesn't? The answer is sitting in the rest of Druckenmiller's portfolio. Druckenmiller funded his quarter one buys by selling. He sold his Libre, he sold his entire Alphabet position, he sold his S&P 500 financial sector ETF. He rotated all of it into three names, memory and storage. The biggest was SanDisk. Druckenmiller opened a new SanDisk position with 38,155 shares. He wasn't alone. David Tepper opened a brand new SanDisk position the same quarter, 281,250 shares. It was the only new position Tepper opened in the first quarter. Out of every stock on the market, he picked one. Two more funds, SRS Investment Management and SCGE Management, also opened new SanDisk positions in Q1. Four separate hedge funds, same brand new position, same quarter. Here is why. SanDisk was spun out of Western Digital last year. It is now the only pure play NAND flash memory company left. And NAND memory just became the bottleneck of the entire AI buildout. SanDisk's third quarter fiscal year 2026 revenue grew 250% year-over-year to $5.95 The company has already locked in over $42 billion in multi-year supply contracts with hyperscalers. SanDisk stock now trades around $1,478, up 3,800% over the past 12 months. Even after that run, four super investors stepped in. They think it is just starting. Two other investors on this list see the exact opposite picture. That is where Howard Marks steps in. Marks runs Oaktree Capital. He is one of the few investors Warren Buffett has publicly said he reads every time he publishes. Oaktree's Q1 13F showed something most coverage missed. AngloGold Ashanti, a gold miner, is Oaktree's third largest disclosed equity position, 7.29% of the entire Q1 book. It also showed something else. Oaktree holds a major position in QQQ puts, a direct bet against the Nasdaq 100. Marks bought gold and he placed a large bearish bet against the same Nasdaq that holds Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, and every other tech stock. He is hedging tech, not buying it. He is betting on what protects you if AI valuations crack. He isn't alone in that camp. Leopold Aschenbrenner, the 26-year-old ex-OpenAI researcher running a fund that grew from $1 billion to $13.6 billion in a year, opened new put positions on Nvidia, Broadcom, Oracle, AMD, and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF in Q1. Aschenbrenner is best known for arguing that artificial general intelligence could arrive by 2027. And now he is buying puts on the chips that make AI possible. Two investors, two completely different career paths, same defensive trade. Gold itself just crossed $5,000 per ounce. Central banks around the world have been buying gold at the fastest pace in decades. While the rest of this cohort is fighting over Alphabet, Microsoft, and SanDisk, Marks and Ashenbrener are quietly building positions on the opposite side. And one investor on this list went even further than that, Mohnish Pabrai. He calls himself a cloner. He built his career studying and copying Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger. Warrior Met Coal, 39.89.89% of his disclosed portfolio. Transocean, 31.97%. Alpha Metallurgical Resources, 28.14%. Coal. Offshore drilling. Coal. 100% of Pabrai's disclosed US equity exposure is in the two most hated sectors of the AI era. Warrior Met produces metallurgical coal, the kind used to make steel. Steel demand has stayed high as the world reindustrializes. Warrior Met has been generating strong free cash flow, paying special dividends, and trading at single-digit price-to-earnings ratios. Transocean drills for oil offshore. Offshore drilling went through a brutal bear market for nearly a decade after the 2015 oil crash. Day rates for offshore drilling rigs have now moved sharply higher again. This is not a tactical trade for Pabrai. This is 40% of his disclosed fund in one coal company. He is betting that the sectors everyone abandoned are where the money gets made next. While Bill Ackman buys Microsoft, while Berkshire buys Google, Druckenmiller buys SanDisk, Pabrai buys coal. One caveat. Pabrai has said most of his real money is in India, Turkey, and South Korea. The US 13F is only what he chose to show, and what he chose to show is 100% coal and offshore drilling.
