# Selah — Master Strategy & Build Document
*Compiled April 2026*

---

## The Big Idea

**Selah is not a Bible app. It is a spiritual disciplines platform with Bible integration.**

The Bible app market is dominated by YouVersion (1 billion+ downloads) but users are deeply frustrated across every major platform. The #1 complaint across ALL apps is inadequate note-taking. The #2 complaint is feature bloat and cluttered interfaces. Nobody has built a complete spiritual disciplines platform. That is the opportunity.

## The Core Problem to Solve

Note-taking is universally broken across every major Bible app:

| App | Note-Taking Problem |
|---|---|
| YouVersion | Buried 3 taps deep, no formatting, users can't find notes after writing them |
| Olive Tree | Note window too small, no bold/bullets/underline |
| Logos | Most powerful but UI so complex most never use it |
| Bible Gateway | Users report losing notes entirely after updates |
| Accordance | Cannot create standalone notes unlinked from a passage |

**The result:** Millions of serious Bible students stitch together YouVersion + Notion + a physical journal just to study Scripture. This is the single clearest signal in the market.

### What "Good" Note-Taking Would Look Like
- Rich text formatting — bold, bullets, headers, underline
- Notes attached to passages AND standalone freeform notes
- Thematic tagging across the whole Bible
- Color-coded highlights with filtering
- Apple Pencil margin writing on iPad
- Search your own notes instantly
- Flawless cross-device sync
- Export to Notion/Obsidian/PDF

## Competitive Landscape

### What Users Love vs. Hate

**YouVersion** — 1 billion+ downloads, completely free
- Love: free, 2,500+ translations, 1,000+ reading plans, streaks
- Hate: note-taking buried, cluttered interface, not for deep study

**Olive Tree** — Best among Bible apps for notes (still falls short)
- Love: cross-device sync, resource guide, user-friendly
- Hate: note window too small, no formatting, no family sharing

**Logos** — Most powerful, worst experience
- Love: Greek/Hebrew tools, huge resource library
- Hate: terrible UI, steep learning curve, expensive, crashes

**Glorify** — 4.79 stars, 1.1M ratings
- Love: soothing narration, simple layout, reduces anxiety
- Hate: feature creep, paywall on previously free features

**Dwell** — Best audio Bible
- Love: natural voices, customizable music, "like Spotify for Bible"
- Hate: $10/month feels expensive, no offline listening

### The False Binary Selah Breaks
The market is trapped between **simple but shallow** (YouVersion) and **powerful but painful** (Logos). No app has successfully served both. Selah's answer is progressive disclosure — beautifully simple by default, depth reveals itself as the user grows.

## Learning Science Applied to Bible Study

The most important insight: **no Bible app is built on learning science**. They're built on access — getting the text in front of you. But access isn't transformation. The gap between reading the Bible and being formed by it is a pedagogical gap, not a content gap.

### How I Was Trained → What You Can Steal

| Training Method | What It Means for Humans |
|---|---|
| Massive diverse exposure | Read widely across domains, not just one lane |
| Repetition with variation | Read multiple authors explaining the same concept |
| Feedback and correction | Seek feedback aggressively — it's the fastest path to growth |
| Learning by failing | Testing yourself before you feel ready wires content in |
| Frameworks applied repeatedly | Learn SOAP, OIA, inductive study — frameworks work on any material |

### Learning Principles Mapped to Bible Study Features

| Learning Principle | Selah Feature |
|---|---|
| Active recall | Post-reading reflection prompts — write what you remember before looking back |
| Spaced repetition | Smart verse memorization — resurface at optimal intervals |
| Elaborative interrogation | "Why" prompts — why do you think God included this here? |
| Generation effect | Pre-reading priming — write what you already believe before reading |
| Teaching others | Study circle share-outs — explain the passage in your own words |
| Emotional encoding | Emotion-based guided sessions — anxiety → Psalm 46 |
| Retrieval practice | SOAP journaling with active prompts, not passive ritual |
| Interleaving | Thematically connected reading plans mixing OT and NT |

### The Three-Step Study Flow (Built & Mocked Up)

**Step 1 — Active Recall**
Before looking back at anything, the user writes what they remember cold. The struggle of retrieval — not re-reading — is what wires content into long-term memory.

**Step 2 — SOAP Journal**
Four tabs: Scripture, Observation, Application, Prayer. Each tab has a specific guiding question — not just a blank box. Specificity moves people from passive journaling to active processing.

**Step 3 — Spaced Repetition**
After studying, user rates how well they know the verse: Hard (resurface in 1 day), Okay (5 days), Easy (14 days). Selah schedules the next review automatically. Nobody has built this properly into a Bible app.

## How to Study the Bible

### Reading Approaches
- **Cover to Cover** — Genesis to Revelation, great for the big picture
- **Book Study** — Deep focus on one book at a time
- **Word/Thematic Study** — Follow a concept (faith, peace, covenant) across Scripture
- **Devotional Reading** — Short daily readings with reflection
- **Lectio Divina** — Slow, meditative, read a short passage multiple times

### Study Frameworks
- **SOAP** — Scripture, Observation, Application, Prayer
- **OIA** — Observe (what does it say?), Interpret (what does it mean?), Apply (what does it mean for me?)
- **Inductive Bible Study** — The seminary standard

### Note-Taking Methods
| Method | Best For |
|---|---|
| SOAP journaling | Daily personal reflection |
| Margin notes | Quick observations while reading |
| Outlining | Book or chapter structure analysis |
| Mind mapping | Thematic or word studies |
| Color-coded highlights | Quick visual categorization |
| Digital notes (synced) | Searchable long-term study records |

## What "Abide" Means (John 15)

A sample of the content Selah's AI system generates:

**The Greek word is *menō* — to remain, stay, dwell, not leave.** Jesus uses it 11 times in just a few verses. The vine image every person in that culture understood instantly: you tended it constantly, depended on it entirely for fruit.

Three identity claims:
- "I am the true vine" — Jesus is the source, the trunk, the life-giving center
- "My Father is the gardener" — God is actively tending and pruning
- "You are the branches" — disciples are extensions, not independent plants

**To abide means staying connected, not straining.** A branch doesn't work to produce grapes — it just stays attached and fruit comes naturally. The question isn't "how do I become more patient?" It's "am I staying close to Jesus?" — and those qualities flow from the connection.

"Apart from me you can do nothing" — not *a little*, not *less*. Nothing. Any spiritual fruit that lasts has its source in him, not human effort alone.

Abiding looks like: opening the Word before the noise starts, praying throughout the day, letting Scripture surface when you face decisions, returning quickly when you've drifted.

**The deepest thing about John 15 is that Jesus initiates it. The vine is already holding the branch. The invitation is simply to stop pulling away.**

## Why Start with John

John is the recommended starting gospel for Selah content for several reasons:

- **Written last, most reflective** — 90% of John's content is unique to John
- **Most theological** — Opens with "In the beginning was the Word," not a birth story
- **Jesus explains himself most directly** — All the "I am" statements are here
- **Most pastoral** — John 14–17 (the Upper Room Discourse) is the richest spiritual formation content in Scripture
- **Aligned with the Abide series** — "Remain in me" from John 15 is Selah's theological heartbeat

After John: Mark (shortest, fastest) → Luke (most compassionate) → Matthew (most structured)

## The Bible Verse Write-Up Generator

### What It Does
A Python script that loops through every verse of the Bible, sends each one to the Claude API with a carefully crafted prompt, and generates a rich pastoral study write-up. Output is saved as JSON organized by book — ready to import into Selah's database.

### Output Structure (Per Verse)
```json
{
  "reference": "John 15:5",
  "book": "John",
  "chapter": 15,
  "verse": 5,
  "text": "I am the vine, you are the branches...",
  "genre": "gospel",
  "headline": "Apart from Jesus, spiritual fruit is impossible",
  "word_study": "The Greek menō (remain/abide) appears 11 times in John 15...",
  "context": "Jesus speaks these words the night before his crucifixion...",
  "meaning": "Jesus is claiming to be the true source of all spiritual life...",
  "application": "Before striving to be more patient or loving, ask first...",
  "connection": "Galatians 5:22–23 lists the fruit of the Spirit...",
  "prayer_prompt": "Lord, help me stay connected to you today instead of straining alone."
}
```

- `test_single.py` — Test one verse before running at scale
- `offline_bible.py` — Download public domain Bible locally
- `requirements.txt` — Python dependencies

### Setup (Mac)
```bash
# 1. Install dependencies
pip3 install -r requirements.txt

# 2. Set API key
export ANTHROPIC_API_KEY=sk-ant-your-key-here

# 3. Fix model name (one-time)
sed -i '' 's/claude-sonnet-4-20250514/claude-sonnet-4-5/g' generate.py test_single.py

# 4. Test one verse
python3 test_single.py

# 5. Run John
python3 generate.py --book John
```

## Three Non-Negotiables

1. **Notes must be first-class.** Not a small pop-up. Notes should feel like the center of the experience — formatted, searchable, organized by theme, synced perfectly.

2. **The interface must be clean by default.** Every feature should be optional and hidden until the user wants it. Should look more like Dwell or Glorify than YouVersion on first launch.

3. **Pricing must feel fair.** Free core + $6–10/month subscription with annual option. Never ads on a paid tier. Never paywall previously free features.

## The Bigger Vision

The app that wins isn't the one with the most features. It's the one that makes a person feel like their spiritual life is *organized, growing, and accompanied* — and does it with a UI beautiful enough that they actually want to open it every day.

Selah has a real opportunity to be the first app that closes the gap between reading the Bible and being formed by it — not by adding more content, but by changing *how* people interact with the content they already have.

That's a genuinely differentiated position. And it aligns with both the mission and the market.
