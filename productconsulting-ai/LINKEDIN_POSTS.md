## Post 1 — The MIT 95% stat, unpacked

> MIT just published a number that should worry every product team shipping AI:
>
> **95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact.**
>
> The first reaction is to blame the models. That's the wrong instinct.
>
> The 5% that work and the 95% that don't are running the same APIs. The difference is upstream of the engineering:
>
> — They picked a real user moment to improve, not a demo to ship.
> — They named the metric the feature had to move, before they wrote a line of code.
> — They scoped it small enough to actually launch, then measure.
>
> When AI features fail, it's almost never the model. It's the three product decisions made before the model showed up.
>
> What are the three decisions on the feature you're building this quarter?

## Post 2 — "What you're NOT building" is the senior move

> Most product reviews I've sat in optimize the wrong question.
>
> The room debates *which* AI feature to build. Sharper teams debate *which AI features to explicitly NOT build*.
>
> The list of "no" — written down, with reasons — is the highest-leverage artifact a product leader produces. It saves a quarter of misspent engineering. It gives the team a defense the next time someone forwards a competitor's announcement.
>
> A few "no" calls I've seen save real money:
>
> — "No to a chatbot. Buy a vendor. Free up the headcount."
> — "No to AI-generated marketing copy as a *product feature.* Buy the marketing team a tool."
> — "No to summarization on top of generic content. Users don't return for a TL;DR button."
>
> If your roadmap doesn't have a visible "not now / not ever" section, you don't have a roadmap. You have a backlog.

## Post 3 — The three questions before any AI feature

> Three questions worth asking before you build any AI feature. Slow questions; fast answers usually mean the wrong answers.
>
> **1. What user moment does this actually improve?**
> Not "users." A moment. The specific instant in their day when this fires. If you can't describe it in one sentence, the feature isn't ready to build.
>
> **2. What metric should move — and by how much?**
> "Engagement" doesn't count. Activation rate, retention, conversion, CSAT, deflection — pick one, name a target. If you can't, you can't tell later whether it worked.
>
> **3. What's the smallest version that proves it?**
> Not the version you want to ship. The version that gives you the signal. Almost always 1/10th of the scope you started with.
>
> AI doesn't change the three questions. It just makes them more expensive to skip.

## Post 4 — The trap of the impressive demo

> Most AI features die between the demo and the user.
>
> The demo is exciting because someone smart curated the inputs. The user is uncurated. They paste broken data, misspell the prompt, hit it while distracted. The model doesn't know it's being tested.
>
> Three things that close the gap:
>
> **1. Build for the edges, not the median.** The median input is easy. The 90th-percentile messy input is where adoption lives or dies.
>
> **2. Always show confidence, never just an answer.** Users can work around an uncertain AI. They can't work around a confident wrong one.
>
> **3. Pair every model output with a path to recover.** Edit, undo, retry, override. If the user is stuck with whatever the model gave them, you've built a toy, not a tool.
>
> A demo is the easy 5%. The other 95% is product work.

## Post 5 — Why AI consulting is broken (and what to do instead)

> The default mode of AI consulting in 2026: a deck, a "transformation roadmap," and a six-figure invoice.
>
> Six months later: the deck is in a drive nobody opens, and the team is still shipping the same product.
>
> Two patterns that actually work, neither of which requires a deck:
>
> **Pattern A: A 2-week, fixed-scope audit** that ranks the team's AI opportunities by ROI, names the build-first, and explicitly lists what NOT to build. A working artifact, not a presentation.
>
> **Pattern B: A fractional product leader** embedded for 2–3 days a week, owning the AI feature work end-to-end. Not advising the work — *doing* the work.
>
> What both have in common: a *senior practitioner in the trenches.* What "transformation programs" have in common: senior people in the kickoff, junior people in the delivery.
>
> If your AI initiative needs a deck before it needs a working prototype, you're already off.

## Post 6 — A short story about scope

> A team I was talking to last month had a six-month "AI assistant" project on the roadmap. Big scope, big timeline, big team carved out.
>
> I asked one question: *if you could ship one tiny piece of this in two weeks and learn something real, what would it be?*
>
> Twenty minutes later they had a different roadmap. Same direction; one-tenth the scope; ten times the chance of shipping.
>
> The thing that kept the original plan in place wasn't ambition. It was the implicit belief that "smaller" meant "less serious."
>
> Counterintuitive truth: **scoping down is the senior move.** Anyone can scope up. It takes judgment to cut the dream into the smallest version that still proves the bet.
>
> If your team's AI roadmap is a 6-month project, you don't have a roadmap. You have a thesis. Test it next sprint.
