# Lead Nurture Sequence — productconsulting.ai

*4 emails sent over 9 days, triggered when someone downloads the framework. The goal isn't to "sell harder" — it's to keep showing up with judgment so the trust compounds. By email 4, booking the teardown feels like the obvious next step.*

**Voice rules (same as posts/ads):**
- Plain text, no fancy templates. Senior B2B buyers read text emails more than designed ones.
- One idea per email. Don't pack.
- One soft link to Cal.com per email, in the body — not a banner CTA.
- Sign with "Amanda" — first name, no title.

**ESP setup (when you wire it):**
- ConvertKit / Buttondown / Tally all support this kind of sequence on free or near-free tiers.
- Trigger: "downloads framework" tag.
- Stop sequence if recipient books a teardown call (you don't want to nurture someone you're about to talk to).

## Email 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** the most common scoring mistake

**Body:**

> Hi {{first_name}},
>
> Quick follow-up on the framework.
>
> The most common mistake people make on first use: they over-weight **Feasibility** and under-weight **Defensibility.**
>
> Why it happens: feasibility is concrete (we have the data, we have the team), and defensibility feels vague (will this matter in two years?). So the easy, copyable features score higher than they deserve.
>
> The result: the team builds a clean AI chatbot in six weeks. It ships. It works. And six months later, every competitor has the same feature, sometimes built better.
>
> The fix is a simple test. For any high-feasibility idea, ask:
>
> *"If we shipped this, and our biggest competitor shipped it three months later, which version would users prefer? Why?"*
>
> If you can't answer, you've found a commodity. Buy it, don't build it.
>
> — Amanda
>
> P.S. — If you want to run this on your actual roadmap with a second pair of eyes, the [free teardown](https://cal.com/amanda-shelton/teardown) is exactly that conversation.

## Email 3 — Day 5

**Subject:** a real teardown (3 min read)

**Body:**

> Hi {{first_name}},
>
> Here's a worked example of the framework on a recognizable category — AI customer support chatbots — so you can see what the scoring looks like with real opinions attached.
>
> **AI Customer Support Chatbot**
>
> ▸ User value: **3 / 5** — solves a real moment, but not one users feel ownership of. They want resolution, not the bot.
> ▸ Business impact: **4 / 5** — deflection rate is a real lever; CSAT is measurable.
> ▸ Feasibility: **5 / 5** — every vendor on the market does this. Trivial to ship.
> ▸ Time to value: **4 / 5** — weeks, not months.
> ▸ Defensibility: **1 / 5** — total commodity. Every competitor will have this in 6 months, sometimes better.
>
> **Total: 17 / 25.** Mixed verdict. The math doesn't say "no" — it says "this is the wrong build."
>
> The right move: **buy a vendor**, not build. Reclaim the engineering quarter for a defensibility-5 feature instead.
>
> That call — "buy don't build" — is what a senior product reviewer is for. The framework just makes it defensible to the team and the board.
>
> — Amanda

## After the sequence
- Tag the subscriber as "completed: framework nurture"
- Move them to a low-frequency list (1 email / month — sharp content from your LinkedIn posts, reformatted as email)
- Anyone who books a teardown automatically exits the sequence (set up in ESP)
- Anyone who replies to any email — flag for personal follow-up

## What I deliberately did NOT do
- No countdown timers, no "act now" urgency
- No fake scarcity ("only 3 spots this month")
- No multi-CTA bombing — one soft Cal.com link per email
- No "I noticed you didn't open my last email" guilt-trip

Senior B2B buyers respect a calm, useful sequence. The aggressive copywriter playbook works for $9 info products. It actively repels a $3K+ buyer.
