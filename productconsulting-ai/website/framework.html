The AI Product Audit Scoring Framework

The opinionated 5-dimension scoring model I use in paid client audits — distilled into a working tool you can apply to your own product this week.

By Amanda Shelton, MBA · Senior Product Manager · productconsulting.ai

Why most AI features quietly fail

95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact (MIT, 2025). That number isn't about model quality — it's about product judgment. Teams pick the wrong bet, scope it so it never ships, or launch features nobody adopts.

Every AI feature is a bet. The job of this framework is to score the bet honestly, before you commit weeks of engineering. The teams that do this well don't have better models — they have better questions.

The 5 dimensions, each scored 1–5

The output isn't "yes/no" — it's a structured argument you can defend to a board, a founder, or yourself at 11pm when you're tempted to chase a shiny demo.

1 User value

Does this improve a real moment in the user's day — measurably?

1 — Cosmetic. Nice demo, no behavior change.
3 — Useful sometimes. Improves a moment users already have.
5 — Hard to give up. Users return weekly for this alone.

2 Business impact

If this works, what specific metric moves — and by how much?

1 — Vague. "Innovation" / "competitive parity."
3 — Lift a secondary metric. Engagement, NPS, CSAT.
5 — Moves the P&L. Revenue, retention, cost, conversion.

3 Feasibility

Can we actually build this with the team, data, and tools we have?

1 — Research-grade. Needs data/teams we don't have.
3 — Doable with hires. Real but solvable gaps.
5 — Off the shelf. Today's APIs and data make it trivial.

4 Time to value

How fast can we ship something that meaningfully tests the bet?

1 — Quarters. Months before any user sees it.
3 — Weeks. A real prototype in 4–6 weeks.
5 — Days. Useful version in under two weeks.

5 Defensibility

Does this compound advantage — or is it a commodity bolt-on?

1 — Commodity. Every competitor will have it in 6 months.
3 — Sticky integration. Tied to your workflow / data.
5 — Compounding moat. Better with usage, hard to copy.

The priority score

Sum all five dimensions for a 5-to-25 score. That number is the start of the conversation — not the end.

Priority = User Value + Business Impact + Feasibility + Time to Value + Defensibility

Each dimension scored 1–5. Maximum 25, minimum 5. Use as a structured tiebreaker, not a god-number.

How to read the total

ScoreVerdictWhat to do
21–25Strong betTighten scope and ship a small version fast.
17–20Real opportunityFix the lowest-scoring dimension before committing.
13–16Mixed — sharpenRework scope or pick a sharper sub-problem.
5–12Likely wrong betConsider a different opportunity entirely.

How to weight by stage and industry

Raw totals are a useful default — but stage and industry should change which dimension you over-index on. A few honest rules of thumb:

  • Seed-stage startup: double-weight Time to Value. You don't have quarters; you have weeks. A "Strong bet" that takes 6 months is a weak bet for you.
  • Scale-up (Series A/B): double-weight Business Impact. You need metrics that justify the next round — not features that look cool in the all-hands.
  • Enterprise product team: double-weight Feasibility and Defensibility. You have data and distribution; play to those, not to demos.
  • Regulated industry (fintech, healthtech, legal): add an implicit safety filter. A high-score idea that can't be explained or audited is a no.

The "what NOT to build" checklist

Counterintuitively, this is the highest-impact part of an audit. Most teams over-index on what they could build and under-index on what they shouldn't. Drop any idea that hits two or more:

  • It scores ≤ 2 on User Value — nobody asked for it; you just want to use the model.
  • It scores ≤ 2 on Defensibility AND is in a commoditized space (chatbots, generic summarization, content gen).
  • You can't name the specific metric it should move within the first month.
  • The team can't articulate the smallest version that proves it works.
  • It needs data you don't have and won't have for a quarter.
  • The success story relies on users behaving differently than they have for years.

A worked example

A SaaS team is debating three AI features. Here's how the framework forces real prioritization:

Idea A — AI customer support chatbot

Total: 14 — Mixed, sharpen or skip

User value 3, Business impact 4, Feasibility 5, Time to value 4, Defensibility 1 (commodity). Easy to build; everyone has one in 6 months. Buy a vendor — don't build.

Idea B — AI-assisted onboarding

Total: 22 — Strong bet

User value 5, Business impact 5, Feasibility 4, Time to value 4, Defensibility 4. Activation is upstream of every downstream metric; the AI version adapts to user inputs, which gets better with usage. Build this one first.

Idea C — Generative content tool for the marketing team

Total: 13 — Sharpen the brief

User value 3, Business impact 3, Feasibility 5, Time to value 4, Defensibility 1 (commodity). Don't build a product feature — buy the marketing team a tool. Reclaim the budget for Idea B.

The framework forced a decision: Idea B is the build-first. The other two get explicit "don't build" verdicts with reasons — saving a quarter of misspent engineering.

How to apply it to your product this week

  1. List 5–9 AI opportunities you've been considering. Be honest, not aspirational.
  2. Score each one against the five dimensions. Use the anchor descriptions above so the scores are defensible, not vibes.
  3. Apply the "what NOT to build" filter first — drop anything that hits two or more criteria.
  4. Pick the highest-scoring survivor and write a one-paragraph build-first plan: smallest valuable version, success metric, target timeline.
  5. Re-score in 30 days. Real data changes the picture. The framework is a tool, not a verdict.
